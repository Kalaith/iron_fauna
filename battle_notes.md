Field the whole party — you're right, and the fix is to stop thinking of the constraint as "how many creatures" and start thinking of it as "there's only one of you." The rider isn't a pilot bolted to one mount; the rider is a single roving force-multiplier the party has to share. That one reframe makes everything you just described click into place.
Here's the model:
The party fights on its own; you make one of them exceptional. You bring 4–6, and they all fight — but only the creature you're currently riding gets your direct control and your boost. Everything you're not on runs autonomously on standing orders (a gambit/tactics profile you set: focus the nearest weapon, guard my core, protect that ally, hold this ground, conserve Vigor). So a party isn't 4–6 fully-piloted units — it's a semi-autonomous pack with one spearhead wherever you happen to be.
The Boost is precision, not just power. Riding a creature gives it two things AI units can't have: manual control — active dodging, positioning, and crucially called shots (aiming at a specific enemy limb or weapon) — plus a signature amp (its strongest graft unlocked, faster Vigor). This is exactly your "I need specific targets" scenario made mechanical: your autonomous units apply broad pressure, but if you need that one artillery weapon stripped off that one enemy, you have to be there — ride the creature that can reach it and take the shot yourself. Only the rider can do surgery.
Repositioning has teeth. Hopping the rider to another creature costs time and exposure — you physically cross to the new mount, and mid-transit you're not shielded behind any core, while both the creature you left and the one you're heading to briefly drop to AI. So you hop when the payoff (a precise kill, a target only that unit can hit, saving a creature about to fall) beats the cost. That's the constant battlefield question: where am I needed most right now?
Now the two places it quietly unifies with what we already built — this is where it gets good:
It fuses with strain. The Boost pushes a creature to its limit, so the one you're actively riding accumulates strain faster. Camp on your favorite creature all fight and it over-strains — rejection, berserk. So you're incentivized to spread your presence across the party, not just for positioning but to let each creature cool off. Rider-hopping becomes strain management, not only tactics. One mechanic, two reasons to do it.
It fuses with the core-crack loss condition. The ridden creature is your highest-value unit — boosted and carrying you — so enemies will focus it. And remember: if the core you're aboard gets cracked while you're on it, you're exposed. That's the danger state. So there's a real "get off the sinking ship" tension: bail from a creature that's about to fall, even mid-plan. Riding makes a creature strong and paints a target on it at the same time.
Two things this forces us to decide, and one honest flag:

This basically answers the combat-structure question from the GDD. "Autonomous units on orders + one you directly control + reposition mid-fight" is real-time-with-pause (or ATB), not clean turn-based. That's a coherent, distinctive identity — but it's a commitment. A turn-based version is possible (AI units take order-driven turns, the ridden one gets a player-directed turn + boost, hopping is an action), just less fluid.
Party size on a 2D side-view field. 4–6 large creatures plus enemies on a flat lane gets crowded and hard to read fast. I'd lean toward ~3 active on the field at once with the rest as a swappable roster, or a lane/zone system to keep it legible. Worth deciding early because it bounds the whole encounter design.
The honest risk: the entire feel rides on the standing-orders AI feeling smart. The mitigation is making orders player-authored and explicit (gambit-style) — then a dumb moment is "my orders were bad," which is fair, rather than "the AI is bad," which isn't.
